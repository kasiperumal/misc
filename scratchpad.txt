## Conversational Struggle Detection System — Summary

The document outlines a patent concept for an AI system that detects when users are struggling in chat-based customer support and intervenes in real time to help them.

**The Core Problem**

Current AI chatbots have no awareness of user frustration. Unlike web tools (e.g., Glassbox) that detect struggle through rage clicks or form errors, chat interfaces have no equivalent signals — only conversation text. This means bots can keep giving unhelpful responses while users get more and more frustrated.

**The Proposed Solution**

A layered system that monitors conversations, calculates a "struggle score" (0–100), and automatically adjusts its responses. It works through six key components:

1. **Intent Engine** — understands what the user is trying to accomplish, even when phrased differently across messages
2. **Task Graph Builder** — maps the conversation as a flow diagram to detect loops and dead ends (the key novel element)
3. **Signal Detection Layer** — identifies repetition, user corrections ("No, that's wrong"), long conversations, and abandonment
4. **Struggle Score Engine** — combines all signals into a single numeric score
5. **Decision Engine** — determines whether to continue normally or trigger an intervention
6. **Intervention Engine** — takes corrective action: clarifying questions, step-by-step guidance, UI shortcuts, or escalating to a human agent

**What Makes It Novel**

The system goes beyond passive analytics — it acts. It also introduces the conversation task graph, which structures dialogue as a journey map rather than a linear transcript, making loop detection possible. It can also retain memory across sessions, recognising returning users who failed previously.

**Business Value**

Fewer support escalations, higher task completion rates, and reduced operational costs — particularly relevant for banking and fintech use cases like PIN resets or KYC flows.

---

**Banking Use Cases**

These are scenarios where the struggle detection system would have immediate, high-value impact in a banking context.

*1. Debit/Credit Card PIN Reset*
A customer locked out of their card tries to reset their PIN via chat. The bot keeps directing them to an ATM, but the customer is travelling abroad and has no ATM access. Without this system, the loop continues until the customer gives up and calls the branch. With it, the task graph detects the ATM → rejection → ATM loop, the score spikes, and the bot pivots to offer a digital PIN reset via the mobile app with OTP verification — resolving the issue without human involvement.

*2. KYC Document Upload Failures*
A customer trying to complete video KYC or upload identity documents repeatedly says "I already uploaded my PAN card." The bot, unable to verify this in real time, keeps asking for the same document. The system detects the contradiction and repetition, intervenes, confirms what has already been received, and clearly tells the user what the next pending step is — for example, address proof or a selfie — eliminating the confusion entirely.

*3. Credit Card Dispute / Chargeback Filing*
A customer wants to dispute a fraudulent transaction but the bot keeps offering generic "block your card" guidance instead of walking them through the dispute process. As the customer repeats "I didn't make this transaction" across multiple turns, the struggle score rises. The system recognises the unresolved intent, shifts approach, and guides the customer step-by-step through raising a chargeback — capturing the right transaction details, timelines, and merchant name — and confirms the dispute reference number at the end.

*4. Loan EMI Deferral or Moratorium Requests*
Customers facing financial hardship often struggle to articulate that they want an EMI pause, not a loan closure or foreclosure. The bot may misclassify intent repeatedly. This system tracks the evolving intent across the conversation, detects that the customer keeps correcting the bot's understanding, and eventually re-routes to a correct, empathetic response — offering moratorium options, eligibility criteria, and application steps clearly.

*5. Fixed Deposit Premature Withdrawal*
A customer wants to break an FD urgently but the chatbot keeps explaining FD renewal options instead. The customer says "No, I need the money now" multiple times. The task graph shows a persistent loop between FD query and renewal responses with no resolution. The system intervenes, acknowledges the premature withdrawal intent, explains the penalty implications clearly, and either completes the action or hands off to a relationship manager if the amount exceeds a threshold.

*6. UPI / NEFT / IMPS Transaction Failure*
A customer's money has been debited but not credited to the beneficiary. They keep asking "where is my money?" and the bot keeps pointing to standard processing timelines. The system detects growing frustration — repetition, corrections, session length — and escalates with a structured response: confirming the debit, raising a transaction trace request, and giving the customer a reference ID and expected resolution timeline rather than leaving them in limbo.

*7. Account Opening Drop-offs*
A new customer is trying to open a savings account digitally but gets stuck at the video KYC step or the nominee declaration section. They abandon and return the next day. The cross-session memory capability of this system recognises the returning user, identifies exactly where they dropped off, and proactively picks up from that step — rather than starting the journey from scratch and causing further frustration.

*8. Salary Account Queries for Corporate Employees*
Employees of a corporate client often ask the same questions in slightly different ways — "when will salary be credited?", "salary not received", "salary account issue" — and get generic responses. The system detects intent clustering around the same unresolved topic, raises the struggle score, and either provides a precise answer based on the employer's salary credit schedule or escalates to the corporate banking team.

---

**How This Differs from Evals and LLM-as-Judge**

At first glance, this system might seem similar to existing evaluation frameworks, but there are fundamental differences across purpose, timing, and mechanism.

*Evals* are offline, batch processes. They assess how well a model performs across a fixed dataset — after conversations have already happened. They answer the question "how good is our model generally?" They do not touch live users and cannot fix anything in the moment. This system, by contrast, operates in real time, within a single live conversation, and its entire purpose is to change the outcome for that specific user right now.

*LLM-as-Judge* is a technique where a separate language model reviews a conversation or response and scores it for quality, relevance, or correctness. While powerful for automated quality assessment, it has three key limitations this system is designed to overcome. First, it is still largely retrospective — judging what was said, not steering what comes next. Second, it evaluates response quality in isolation, not user journey progression. A response can be linguistically correct and still leave a user completely stuck in a loop, which LLM-as-Judge would likely miss. Third, it has no intervention capability — it produces a verdict, not an action.

This system is fundamentally different because it is **diagnostic and prescriptive, not just evaluative**. The struggle score is not meant to grade the bot — it is a trigger for changing behaviour mid-conversation. The task graph adds a structural layer that neither evals nor LLM-as-judge use: it tracks *where the user is in their journey*, not just *what was said*. That distinction — journey state versus response quality — is what allows this system to catch failure patterns like loops and dead ends that traditional evaluation methods are blind to.

In short: evals and LLM-as-judge tell you what went wrong after the fact. This system detects it as it unfolds and does something about it.
